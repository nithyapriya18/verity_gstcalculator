When someone comes to me with a project, the first thing I do is not write a proposal.

I don't ask for a brief. I don't send over a list of questions. I don't open my laptop and start thinking about the technology.

I ask for 90 minutes of their time. And I bring a blank page.

## The conversation they haven't had yet

Here's what usually happens in that 90 minutes.

In the first thirty, the person explains what they want. A tool that does X. An automation that handles Y. A dashboard that shows Z.

I listen and I take notes.

Then, around the forty-minute mark, I start asking different questions. Not about the solution — about the problem.

"Walk me through what happens when the thing doesn't exist. What does a Monday look like?"

"Who is the last person to touch this before it gets done?"

"If I built exactly what you described, and it worked perfectly, what would be different six months from now?"

These questions change the conversation.

Because most people, when they've been living with a problem long enough, have already formed a solution in their head. And they come to me wanting that solution built.

But the solution in their head isn't always the right one. It's just the most obvious one.

## What discovery is actually for

Discovery isn't a formality. It's not a box I tick before I start writing code.

It's the part where I figure out what the problem actually is.

Nine times out of ten, the thing someone asks me to build is not the thing they need. Not because they're wrong — they know their business better than I do. But because the most obvious solution is often a local maximum.

It fixes the symptom. It doesn't fix the cause.

An example: A client came to me wanting a better reporting tool. They were exporting data to Excel, cleaning it manually, and sending it as an email attachment every Friday. They wanted me to build something that automated the Excel export.

That would have worked. But after 90 minutes, I understood the real problem.

The report existed because their clients needed to know certain things. The clients didn't actually care about the format. They just needed to know the things.

We didn't automate the Excel export. We built a client portal where the information updated in real time.

The problem was communication. The spreadsheet was just the bandage.

## What the blank page is for

I bring a blank page — sometimes literally, sometimes a blank document — because it forces me to start from nothing.

I'm not filling in a template. I'm not checking boxes. I'm listening and trying to understand.

At the end of the 90 minutes, I have notes. And from those notes, I write one page: what the problem is, what I'd build to solve it, how long it would take, and what it would cost.

One page. Not a 40-slide deck.

If I've done the 90 minutes well, that one page will feel obvious to the person reading it. Like I took what was in their head and wrote it down.

That's what good discovery feels like.

## The shortcut that isn't

I know this sounds slow. Ninety minutes before anything else?

But consider the alternative.

You skip the conversation. You build what was asked. Three weeks later, the thing works — but it doesn't solve the problem.

Then comes the hard conversation. The one about scope and changes and "I thought it would work differently."

I've learned that 90 minutes at the start saves weeks at the end.

It's not a delay. It's the work.
